🌞 1. Physical World (Analog Light)

Light travels as continuous electromagnetic waves with a spectrum of wavelengths. Our surroundings reflect these rays of light continuously into the lens.

📷 2. Camera Sensor (Bayer Grid)

Photodiodes capture brightness at discrete grid coordinates (Spatial Sampling). A colored filter array (Bayer Filter) measures color channels in a GRGB layout.

Brightness values are converted to integers, e.g., 0-255 (Quantization). The resulting 3D matrix (tensor) is loaded into memory, ready for computer vision and AI models.

What is Light? (Physics of Vision)
How nature produces what computers measure
Everyday Hook

Nong, a biology student, points his phone at cells under a microscope. The light passes through the glass, hits the cell membrane, reflects into the camera lens, and generates a picture on his screen.

Analogy

Human Cones & Rods are like hardware subpixels. Our retina contains Rods (grayscale brightness) and Cones (Blue, Green, Red wavelength sensors). The camera copies this with color filters.

Misconception

Students often think: A camera sensor captures colors directly in a single pixel.
But actually: Sensors are color-blind! They only measure raw light energy. To capture color, they use a pattern of red, green, and blue plastic filters over different photodiodes.

The Image Digitization Grid
How continuous light turns into pixel tensors
1. Spatial Sampling (The Pixel Grid)

Continuous physical images are projected onto a grid of photodiodes. Each photodiode gathers photons in its area, representing a pixel. This defines the spatial resolution ($H \times W$).

2. Color Sensing (The Bayer Filter)

Most cameras use a Bayer Filter Array. In an $8\times 8$ grid, 50% are Green, 25% are Red, and 25% are Blue (GRGB pattern). We have twice as many green pixels because human eyes are far more sensitive to green luminance!

3. Demosaicing (Color Reconstruction)

Since each sensor cell captures only 1 color channel, computer algorithms interpolate neighboring values to produce full 3-channel values (R, G, and B) for every pixel.

Checkpoint: Predict Pixel & Bit Sizes
Test your understanding of sensor math and sizes
1. A 12-Megapixel camera has a resolution of 4000x3000 pixels. How many green photodiodes are actually on its Bayer sensor grid?
Answer: 6 million pixels. Since the standard Bayer Filter Array contains 50% Green, 25% Red, and 25% Blue filters, exactly half of the sensor's photodiodes are covered by green filters ($12\text{ million} \times 0.5 = 6\text{ million}$).
2. If we use a 10-bit analog-to-digital converter (ADC) on each photodiode, how many discrete levels of brightness can the sensor record?
Answer: $2^{10} = 1024$ levels (ranging from 0 to 1023). Most standard images use 8-bit quantization ($2^8 = 256$ levels), while professional RAW camera files often use 12-bit ($4096$ levels) or 14-bit ($16384$ levels) for dynamic range.
3. If we load an uncompressed 8-bit color image of 4000x3000 pixels into a NumPy tensor, what is its size in bytes?
Answer: $36,000,000$ bytes (or $36.0\text{ MB}$ in decimal, $34.33\text{ MiB}$ in binary).
Calculated as: $\text{Width} \times \text{Height} \times \text{Channels} \times \text{Bytes-per-pixel} = 4000 \times 3000 \times 3\text{ channels} \times 1\text{ byte} = 36\text{ million bytes}$.

Grayscale & Perception
Why we do not just average R, G, and B
Why Simple Averaging Fails

If we use $Y = \frac{R + G + B}{3}$, we treat Red, Green, and Blue equally.
However, under the exact same light intensity, pure Green feels much brighter to human eyes than pure Blue. Averaging results in grays that look visually wrong.

The BT.601 Luminance Formula

To match human brightness perception, we apply different weights to color channels:

$$Y = 0.299 \cdot R + 0.587 \cdot G + 0.114 \cdot B$$

Note that Green accounts for **$58.7\%$** of the brightness, while Blue accounts for only **$11.4\%$**.

Worked Example: Converting Pathology Stain Color

Suppose Nong measures a stain tissue color with RGB values of $(120, 200, 50)$ (a bright light-green tint).

1. Simple Average Method:

$Y_{\text{avg}} = \frac{120 + 200 + 50}{3} = \frac{370}{3} \approx 123.33$

2. BT.601 Luminance Method:

$Y_{\text{BT601}} = 0.299(120) + 0.587(200) + 0.114(50) = 35.88 + 117.40 + 5.70 = 158.98$

The BT.601 result of **159** is significantly brighter than the simple average of **123**, reflecting the human visual system's high sensitivity to the green light present in the pixel.

Quick Check: If RGB = (0, 0, 255) (pure Blue), what is the BT.601 luminance?
$Y = 0.299(0) + 0.587(0) + 0.114(255) = 29.07$. Pure blue has a very low perceived brightness of only **29** out of 255.

Understanding Histograms
Analyzing image brightness, exposure, and contrast
📉 What is a Histogram?

A graph plotting brightness intensity (X-axis: 0 to 255) against the number of pixels possessing that intensity value (Y-axis).

🌗 Exposure Diagnostic Shapes

- Underexposed (Too Dark): Peaks are clustered heavily on the left side (close to 0).
- Overexposed (Too Bright): Peaks are clustered heavily on the right side (close to 255).

⚖️ Contrast Diagnostic Shapes

- Low Contrast: A narrow, high spike in the middle of the graph (no deep blacks or bright whites).
- High Contrast: Peaks at both extreme ends (high counts of pure black and pure white).

Checkpoint: Histogram & Grayscale
Evaluate your knowledge of grayscale math and histograms
1. Calculate the BT.601 Grayscale value for a microscopic cell pixel having RGB = $(100, 200, 50)$.
Answer: $153$ (exactly $153.0$).
Calculated as: $Y = 0.299(100) + 0.587(200) + 0.114(50) = 29.9 + 117.4 + 5.7 = 153.0$.
2. An image has a histogram where all counts are concentrated tightly between 110 and 140. How does this image look?
Answer: It looks flat, grayish, and has **low contrast**. Since all pixel values are very close together in the medium gray range, there are no bright whites or deep blacks to create contrast.
3. If we swap the channels of a color image from RGB to BGR, does the grayscale histogram of the image change?
Answer: Yes, it does! Because the BT.601 formula weights Red (29.9%) and Blue (11.4%) differently. If we swap the channel positions without updating the weights, the calculated luminance values change, which shifts the grayscale histogram. (To keep the grayscale image the same, we must swap the multipliers accordingly).

Basic Tensor Transforms
Applying math equations to NumPy pixel arrays
💡 Brightness & Contrast Adjustments

- Brightness ($\beta$): Shift by offset $I_{\text{new}} = I + \beta$. Adding $\beta > 0$ brightens, subtracting $\beta < 0$ darkens.
- Contrast ($\alpha$): Scale by factor $I_{\text{new}} = \alpha \cdot I$. $\alpha > 1$ increases contrast, $\alpha < 1$ compresses it.

⚠️ The Overflow Trap

Standard image arrays use `uint8` (0-255). Adding 10 to a pixel of 250 leads to $260 \rightarrow 4$ (wraps around). We must cast to float, calculate, **clip to 0-255**, and cast back.

Linear Enhancement Equation
$$I_{\text{new}}(y, x) = \text{clip}\big(\alpha \cdot I(y, x) + \beta,\ 0,\ 255\big)$$
NumPy Syntax Execution
# Safe math with type-casting and clipping
enhanced = np.clip(alpha * img.astype(np.float32) + beta, 0, 255).astype(np.uint8)
What happens without clipping?
If $I = 200$ and we scale contrast by $\alpha = 1.4$ under `uint8` math:
$200 \times 1.4 = 280 \rightarrow 280 - 256 = 24$ (wraps around).
Instead of becoming brighter, this highlight pixel **turns dark**! Clipping keeps it at $255$.

Slicing & Swapping Channels
Manipulating the third dimension of image tensors
1. Color Channel Swapping

Most libraries (like PIL or PyTorch) load images as RGB. However, OpenCV loads them as BGR. To convert between them, we swap the depth indices: 

img_rgb = img_bgr[:, :, ::-1]
2. Tensor Cropping (ROI Slicing)

To extract a subregion from a larger tensor, we slice the height and width axes directly: 

cropped_patch = img[y_start:y_end, x_start:x_end, :]

Mini-Workshop: NumPy Pixel Manipulator
Load and modify images directly using Python array operations
import numpy as np
import cv2

# 1. Load color image as 3D NumPy array (BGR)
img_bgr = cv2.imread("cell_stain.png")
H, W, C = img_bgr.shape

# 2. Swap color channels to RGB
img_rgb = img_bgr[:, :, ::-1]

# 3. Brightness & Contrast: alpha=1.3, beta=20
enhanced = np.clip(1.3 * img_rgb.astype(np.float32) + 20, 0, 255).astype(np.uint8)

# 4. Crop center 100x100 pixel region
sy, sx = (H - 100) // 2, (W - 100) // 2
crop = enhanced[sy:sy+100, sx:sx+100, :]

💡 Practical Tips

Do not swap channels manually using nested loops! Slicing with `::-1` is extremely fast because it runs in C-code vector optimization and creates an instant view reference rather than making a copy of the elements.

If a crop runs `img[50:150, 50:150, :]`, what is the shape of the result?
The shape is exactly `(100, 100, 3)`. The crop selects 100 rows and 100 columns across all 3 color channels.
